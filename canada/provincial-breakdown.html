Why Saskatchewan first

Saskatchewan's Pharmaceutical Information Program (PIP), managed by eHealth Saskatchewan, captures a near-complete record of prescription activity across the province. That breadth of data creates a uniquely clear integration target — PIP already knows what was dispensed. Medisyns adds what PIP cannot provide: real-time payer-side verification at the moment of dispensing, and a fraud-flag layer that cross-references prescriber authorization, patient eligibility, and benefit duplication in a single event.

The fraud ROI case in Saskatchewan is among the strongest in Canada. At a conservative 2% fraud loss on ~$833M in annual drug plan spend, the exposure exceeds $16M per year — more than double the cost of a full Medisyns deployment at pilot scale. The province doesn't need to believe a high fraud estimate for the business case to hold. The conservative case is already compelling.

The procurement path

Gaining programmatic API access to PIP is a procurement conversation with eHealth Saskatchewan, not a purely technical integration. Medisyns should be positioned as an enhancement layer that increases PIP's value — not a competing system. The SHA is the primary relationship; eHealth Saskatchewan is the technical counterpart.

The goal of initial engagement is a designated implementation contact within the SHA and a defined pilot scope, not a full provincial procurement commitment at first contact.

Why Ontario is the scale test

Ontario has already built the proof of concept for every component of what Medisyns does — in isolation. The Digital Health Drug Repository (DHDR), the Health Network System (HNS), and the Narcotics Monitoring System (NMS) each verify a different layer of the prescription chain. What Ontario has never built is a protocol that connects them into a single real-time event.

The Ontario pitch is not "here's a new idea." It's "you've already proven the value of each piece separately — here's the layer that unifies them." That framing removes the innovation risk objection entirely: Ontario has already invested in the infrastructure that Medisyns integrates.

The second angle is coordination of benefits. Ontario's 8 million dual-insured patients — those holding both OHIP coverage and a private employer plan — represent the single largest unresolved real-time verification gap in Canadian healthcare. No system today confirms, in a single event, that both payers have been correctly billed and that no duplication has occurred.

The Ontario entry point

A full Ontario Ministry of Health procurement is not the entry point. The fastest path is coordination of benefits between ODB and private insurers — a gap that exists independent of full ministry engagement and where the stakeholder universe is smaller.

The NMS provides a precedent: Ontario has already accepted the principle of cross-system monitoring for controlled substances. Medisyns extends that logic to the full formulary and to the payer-side verification gap.

Why BC anchors the national model

British Columbia's PharmaNet is the most mature provincial prescription database in Canada. Operational since 1995, it captures every prescription dispensed in the province in near real-time — a capability that most other provinces are still building toward. That maturity makes BC an ideal integration target and a proving ground for what a Medisyns national rollout looks like at scale.

BC's sophistication also means the Medisyns pitch is different here than in Saskatchewan or Ontario. The province is not being sold on the value of tracking prescriptions — they've been doing that for 30 years. The pitch is specifically about the payer-side fraud layer that PharmaNet does not provide: real-time verification that the prescriber is authorized, the patient is eligible under the correct benefit, and the claim is not being duplicated across payers.

A successful BC integration — following Saskatchewan and Ontario — creates the three-province precedent that Canada Health Infoway needs to formalize Medisyns as a national standard. BC is strategically the most important Phase 3 target for that reason.

The national standard pathway

Three provincial adoptions — Saskatchewan, Ontario, BC — spanning eastern and western Canada create the evidence base for Canada Health Infoway to designate Medisyns as a national prescription verification standard. No individual province needs to carry the weight of a national procurement decision. The standard emerges from adoption.

Alberta and Manitoba are identified as the next expansion targets following BC, completing the western Canada coverage and the conditions for full Infoway engagement.
