6.7% of pharmacies inspected — $9.1M recovered, $3.9M missed
286 of 4,260

In 2016/17, the Ontario Ministry of Health inspected just 286 of more than 4,260 pharmacies — 6.7% of the total. Those inspections recovered $9.1 million in inappropriate claims. The Auditor General separately identified a further $3.9 million in inappropriate claims the Ministry did not inspect or recover. The Ministry was found to lack an overall inspection strategy to target high-risk pharmacies.

Source Office of the Auditor General of Ontario, 2017 Annual Report, Section 3.09: Ontario Public Drug Programs (Value-for-Money Audit). Audit period: 2016/17 fiscal year. auditor.on.ca
2
$5.4B ODB program with no systematic high-risk inspection strategy
$5.4 billion

Total expenditures of the Ontario Drug Benefit Program in 2016/17 were approximately $5.4 billion, serving approximately 4 million recipients. The Auditor General concluded the Ministry was not recovering many invalid claims and payments to pharmacists. Against a program of this scale, even a fraction of a percent in fraud or inappropriate billing represents tens of millions of dollars of undetected losses.

Source Office of the Auditor General of Ontario, 2017 Annual Report, Section 3.09: Ontario Public Drug Programs. auditor.on.ca
3
245,000 opioid tablets vanished from one Ontario pharmacy — official reason: "Loss unexplained"
$4M street value

In May 2023, a pharmacy somewhere in Ontario reported the loss of more than 245,000 hydromorphone (Dilaudid) tablets to Health Canada. There was no robbery and no break-in. The official reason recorded by Health Canada: "Loss unexplained." There is no legal requirement to report such losses to police. The Ontario College of Pharmacists launched an investigation but could not provide further details.

Source CBC News investigation, June 27, 2024. Data obtained from Health Canada via access-to-information request. Health Canada data confirmed authentic by Health Canada spokesperson. cbc.ca
4
Ontario government acknowledges duplicate prescribing as an active fraud vector with no real-time solution
No pre-dispense detection

The Ontario government's own fraud prevention page acknowledges duplicate prescriptions — obtaining the same drug from multiple pharmacies — and "double doctoring" (obtaining opioid prescriptions from three or more doctors in a month) as active fraud vectors in the ODB program. No cross-system real-time verification layer exists that would catch these events before dispensing occurs.

Source Government of Ontario, Preventing Fraud Within Ontario's Health Care System. ontario.ca

What Medisyns provides for Ontario

Real-time cross-system verification the ODB program currently lacks

The Auditor General found Ontario inspects 6.7% of pharmacies and still missed $3.9M in inappropriate claims. The government's own fraud page acknowledges duplicate prescriptions and double-doctoring as known problems — with no real-time solution. Medisyns connects the ODB system, private insurers, prescribers, and pharmacies. Every fill is cross-checked before it is dispensed. Duplication is caught at authorization, not discovered in a retrospective audit years later.
