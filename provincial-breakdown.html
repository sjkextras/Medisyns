SK
Saskatchewan
Population: 1.2M · Saskatchewan Health Authority · Ministry of Health
1
No active monitoring of pharmacy dispensing practices
Zero

The Saskatchewan Ministry of Health does not actively monitor the dispensing practices of the province's 385 pharmacies, and does not know whether those practices contribute to prescription drug misuse. A risk-based monitoring approach does not exist. There is no mechanism to detect whether pharmacies are properly dispensing opioids or other controlled substances.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7: Health—Monitoring Opioid Prescribing and Dispensing. auditor.sk.ca · Audit period: March 1, 2018 – February 28, 2019
2
Saskatchewan opioid prescribing significantly above national average
6,616 vs 5,479

For the six most commonly prescribed opioids, Saskatchewan's defined daily doses of 6,616 per 1,000 population is well above the national average of 5,479 per 1,000 population — a 21% premium over the national rate. Physicians prescribe almost all opioids in the province. The Provincial Auditor found the Ministry did not monitor all opioids prescribed in Saskatchewan, including ones known to cause overdose or death.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7. Data sourced from Canadian Institute for Health Information, Pan-Canadian Trends in the Prescribing of Opioids and Benzodiazepines, 2012 to 2017, p. 20. auditor.sk.ca
3
Regina and Saskatoon — highest opioid hospitalization rates in Canada
28.3 per 100,000

Among 19 Canadian cities with populations over 100,000, Regina ranked first for opioid poisoning hospitalization rates at 28.3 per 100,000 people in 2016–17. Saskatoon ranked second at 26.1 per 100,000. Both are dramatically higher than Toronto (7.9) and Vancouver (20.5). The Provincial Auditor linked these rates directly to the absence of adequate prescribing and dispensing monitoring.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7. Original data: Canadian Institute for Health Information, Pan-Canadian Trends in the Prescribing of Opioids and Benzodiazepines, 2012 to 2017, p. 33. auditor.sk.ca
4
441,354 opioid prescriptions filled — majority unmonitored at dispensing
441,354

In the 2018–19 fiscal year, Saskatchewan pharmacies filled 441,354 opioid prescriptions for 98,947 individuals. The majority were for hydromorphone (45%), codeine (32%), and morphine (10%). A further 359,681 prescriptions were dispensed for opioid addiction treatment. None of these dispensing events were subject to active provincial monitoring at the pharmacy level.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7. Data provided by the Ministry of Health. auditor.sk.ca
📅

Data currency note: The Saskatchewan Provincial Auditor's most recent performance audit of opioid prescribing and dispensing monitoring was published in 2019 (audit period ending February 2019). The 2024 and 2025 Auditor reports do not contain updated drug plan monitoring findings. The 2019 audit remains the most recent primary government source on this topic. Medisyns does not extrapolate or update these figures — they are presented as audited.

ON
Ontario
Population: 15M · Ontario Drug Benefit Program · Ministry of Health
1
6.7% of pharmacies inspected — $9.1M in inappropriate claims recovered
286 of 4,260

In 2016/17, the Ontario Ministry of Health inspected just 286 of more than 4,260 pharmacies — 6.7% of the total. Those inspections recovered $9.1 million in inappropriate claims. The Auditor General separately identified a further $3.9 million in inappropriate claims that the Ministry did not inspect or recover. The Ministry was found to lack an overall inspection strategy to target high-risk pharmacies.

Source Office of the Auditor General of Ontario, 2017 Annual Report, Section 3.09: Ontario Public Drug Programs (Value-for-Money Audit). auditor.on.ca · Audit period: 2016/17 fiscal year
2
$5.4 billion ODB program with no systematic high-risk inspection strategy
$5.4B

Total expenditures of the Ontario Drug Benefit Program in 2016/17 were approximately $5.4 billion (including co-payments and deductibles), serving approximately 4 million recipients. The Auditor General concluded the Ministry was not recovering many invalid claims and payments to pharmacists — against a program of this size, even a fraction of a percent in fraud or inappropriate billing represents tens of millions of dollars.

Source Office of the Auditor General of Ontario, 2017 Annual Report, Section 3.09: Ontario Public Drug Programs. auditor.on.ca
3
245,000 opioid tablets disappeared from one Ontario pharmacy — reason: "unexplained"
$4M street value

In May 2023, a pharmacy somewhere in Ontario reported the loss of more than 245,000 hydromorphone (Dilaudid) tablets to Health Canada. There was no robbery and no break-in. The manufacturer reported no supply chain concerns. The official reason recorded by Health Canada: "Loss unexplained." There is no requirement to report such losses to police. The Ontario College of Pharmacists launched an investigation but could not provide further details.

Source CBC News investigation, June 27, 2024. Data obtained from Health Canada via access-to-information request. cbc.ca/news/canada/unexplained-losses-prescription-drugs-1.7247602
4
Ontario government acknowledges duplicate prescribing and double-doctoring as active fraud vectors
No real-time detection

The Ontario government's own public fraud prevention page acknowledges that duplicate prescriptions — obtaining the same drug from multiple pharmacies — and "double doctoring" (obtaining opioid prescriptions from three or more doctors in a month) are active fraud vectors in the ODB program. The Health Network System provides some data to pharmacists, but there is no cross-system real-time verification layer that would catch these events before dispensing occurs.

Source Government of Ontario, Preventing Fraud Within Ontario's Health Care System. ontario.ca/page/preventing-fraud-within-ontarios-health-care-system
📅

Data currency note: The Ontario Auditor General's most recent Value-for-Money audit of the Ontario Drug Benefit Program was published in 2017 (audit period 2016/17). The pharmacy inspection figures (286 of 4,260) and recovery amounts ($9.1M) reflect that audit period. Medisyns presents these as the most recent audited figures available from a primary government source. The 2023 CBC News/Health Canada data is current as of the publication date.

What Medisyns provides for Ontario

Real-time cross-system verification the ODB program currently lacks

The Auditor General found Ontario inspects 6.7% of pharmacies and still missed $3.9M in inappropriate claims. The government's own fraud page acknowledges duplicate prescriptions and double-doctoring as known problems — with no real-time solution. Medisyns sits between the ODB system, private insurers, prescribers, and pharmacies. Every fill is cross-checked before it is dispensed. Duplication is caught at the point of authorization, not discovered in a retrospective audit years later.
