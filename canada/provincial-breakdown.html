Saskatchewan
Active engagement

Saskatchewan's Pharmaceutical Information Program (PIP) captures a near-complete provincial prescription record — the most advanced integration foundation of any active target. Medisyns adds the one layer PIP doesn't provide: real-time payer-side verification at the point of dispensing. The Saskatchewan Health Authority has escalated materials to senior decision-makers. At a conservative 2% fraud rate on the $833M public drug plan, the exposure exceeds $16M annually — more than double the cost of full deployment.

Ontario
In pipeline

Ontario has already built each verification layer in isolation — DHDR for dispensing records, HNS for ODB eligibility, NMS for narcotics monitoring. None of them talk to each other. With 8M+ dual-insured patients and pharmacies averaging 15 years between inspections, Ontario's coordination of benefits gap is the single largest unresolved verification problem in Canadian healthcare. Medisyns is the protocol that unifies what Ontario has already proven piece by piece.

British Columbia
In pipeline

BC's PharmaNet is the most mature provincial prescription database in Canada — operational since 1995, capturing every prescription dispensed in the province in near real-time. That maturity makes BC the ideal proving ground for what a national Medisyns rollout looks like at scale. The pitch here isn't about the value of tracking prescriptions — BC has done that for 30 years. It's about the payer-side fraud layer PharmaNet has never had.

Alberta
Next phase

Alberta accounts for a disproportionate share of Canada's opioid crisis — 1,870 apparent opioid toxicity deaths in 2023 alone, the province's deadliest year on record. Alberta Health Services administers a large public drug program through Alberta Blue Cross with no dedicated pharmacy dispensing audit infrastructure. The Danielle Smith government's recovery-oriented, fraud-prevention posture makes Alberta ideologically aligned with the Medisyns pitch in a way that other provinces may not be.

Manitoba
Next phase

The College of Pharmacists of Manitoba is actively warning pharmacists about a high volume of prescription forgeries — Percocet and Cotridin are the most common targets, with forged prescriptions being faxed from out-of-province numbers at scale. Manitoba's Prescribing Practices Program (M3P) is prescriber-focused, not dispensing-focused. That's exactly Medisyns' gap: the moment between a prescription being issued and a pharmacist dispensing it has no cross-system verification.
