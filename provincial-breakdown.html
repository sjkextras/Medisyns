SK
Saskatchewan
Population: 1.2M · Saskatchewan Health Authority · Ministry of Health
1
No active monitoring of pharmacy dispensing practices
Zero monitoring

The Saskatchewan Ministry of Health does not actively monitor the dispensing practices of the province's 385 pharmacies, and does not know whether those practices contribute to prescription drug misuse. A risk-based monitoring approach does not exist. There is no mechanism to detect whether pharmacies are properly dispensing opioids or other controlled substances.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7: Health—Monitoring Opioid Prescribing and Dispensing. Audit period: March 1, 2018 – February 28, 2019. auditor.sk.ca
2
Opioid prescribing 21% above the national average
6,616 vs 5,479

For the six most commonly prescribed opioids, Saskatchewan's defined daily doses of 6,616 per 1,000 population is well above the national average of 5,479 per 1,000 population — a 21% premium over the national rate. The Provincial Auditor found the Ministry did not monitor all opioids prescribed in Saskatchewan, including ones known to cause overdose or death.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7. Original data: Canadian Institute for Health Information, Pan-Canadian Trends in the Prescribing of Opioids and Benzodiazepines, 2012–2017, p.20. auditor.sk.ca
3
Regina #1 and Saskatoon #2 in Canada for opioid hospitalization rates
28.3 per 100,000

Among 19 Canadian cities with populations over 100,000, Regina ranked first for opioid poisoning hospitalization rates at 28.3 per 100,000 people in 2016–17. Saskatoon ranked second at 26.1 per 100,000. Both are dramatically higher than Toronto (7.9) and Vancouver (20.5). The Provincial Auditor linked these rates directly to the absence of adequate prescribing and dispensing monitoring.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7. Original data: CIHI, Pan-Canadian Trends in the Prescribing of Opioids and Benzodiazepines, 2012–2017, p.33. auditor.sk.ca
4
441,354 opioid prescriptions filled — all unmonitored at the dispensing level
441,354

In 2018–19, Saskatchewan pharmacies filled 441,354 opioid prescriptions for 98,947 individuals. The majority were hydromorphone (45%), codeine (32%), and morphine (10%). A further 359,681 prescriptions were dispensed for opioid addiction treatment. None of these dispensing events were subject to active provincial monitoring at the pharmacy level.

Source Provincial Auditor of Saskatchewan, 2019 Report – Volume 1, Chapter 7. Data provided by the Saskatchewan Ministry of Health. auditor.sk.ca
📅

Data currency note: The most recent Saskatchewan Provincial Auditor performance audit of opioid prescribing and dispensing was published in 2019 (audit period ending February 2019). The 2024 and 2025 Auditor reports do not contain updated drug plan monitoring findings. These figures are presented as audited — Medisyns does not extrapolate or update them.

ON
Ontario
Population: 15M · Ontario Drug Benefit Program · Ministry of Health
1
6.7% of pharmacies inspected — $9.1M recovered, $3.9M missed
286 of 4,260

In 2016/17, the Ontario Ministry of Health inspected just 286 of more than 4,260 pharmacies — 6.7% of the total. Those inspections recovered $9.1 million in inappropriate claims. The Auditor General separately identified a further $3.9 million in inappropriate claims the Ministry did not inspect or recover. The Ministry was found to lack an overall inspection strategy to target high-risk pharmacies.

Source Office of the Auditor General of Ontario, 2017 Annual Report, Section 3.09: Ontario Public Drug Programs (Value-for-Money Audit). Audit period: 2016/17 fiscal year. auditor.on.ca
2
$5.4B ODB program with no systematic high-risk inspection strategy
$5.4 billion

Total expenditures of the Ontario Drug Benefit Program in 2016/17 were approximately $5.4 billion, serving approximately 4 million recipients. The Auditor General concluded the Ministry was not recovering many invalid claims and payments to pharmacists. Against a program of this scale, even a fraction of a percent in fraud or inappropriate billing represents tens of millions of dollars of undetected losses.

Source Office of the Auditor General of Ontario, 2017 Annual Report, Section 3.09: Ontario Public Drug Programs. auditor.on.ca
3
245,000 opioid tablets vanished from one Ontario pharmacy — official reason: "Loss unexplained"
$4M street value

In May 2023, a pharmacy somewhere in Ontario reported the loss of more than 245,000 hydromorphone (Dilaudid) tablets to Health Canada. There was no robbery and no break-in. The official reason recorded by Health Canada: "Loss unexplained." There is no legal requirement to report such losses to police. The Ontario College of Pharmacists launched an investigation but could not provide further details.

Source CBC News investigation, June 27, 2024. Data obtained from Health Canada via access-to-information request. Health Canada data confirmed authentic by Health Canada spokesperson. cbc.ca
4
Ontario government acknowledges duplicate prescribing as an active fraud vector with no real-time solution
No pre-dispense detection

The Ontario government's own fraud prevention page acknowledges duplicate prescriptions — obtaining the same drug from multiple pharmacies — and "double doctoring" (obtaining opioid prescriptions from three or more doctors in a month) as active fraud vectors in the ODB program. No cross-system real-time verification layer exists that would catch these events before dispensing occurs.

Source Government of Ontario, Preventing Fraud Within Ontario's Health Care System. ontario.ca
📅

Data currency note: The Ontario Auditor General's most recent Value-for-Money audit of the Ontario Drug Benefit Program was published in 2017 (audit period 2016/17). The pharmacy inspection figures and recovery amounts reflect that audit period and are the most recent audited figures available from a primary government source. The 2023 CBC News/Health Canada data is current as of the publication date.

BC
British Columbia
Population: 5.5M · BC PharmaCare · Ministry of Health · PharmaNet
1
BC Ministry of Health confirms "significant portion" of prescribed opioids being diverted
Significant diversion

A BC Ministry of Health internal investigative unit briefing, confirmed authentic by Health Minister Josie Osborne in February 2025, states that a "significant portion" of prescribed opioids in BC are being diverted and that prescribed alternatives are being trafficked provincially, nationally, and internationally. The investigation involves organized criminals, doctors, assisted living residences, and pharmacies.

Source BC Ministry of Health investigative unit briefing, confirmed by Health Minister Josie Osborne. Government news release: February 19, 2025. news.gov.bc.ca · CBC News coverage confirmed by minister: cbc.ca
2
More than 60 pharmacies under investigation for kickback and overbilling schemes
60+ pharmacies

Since 2024, the BC Ministry of Health's Special Investigative Unit, in collaboration with the College of Pharmacists of BC and law enforcement, has been investigating pharmacies suspected of illegal activities — including misusing fee-for-service payments to offer kickbacks to attract patients, and filling prescriptions patients did not require in order to overbill PharmaCare. More than 60 pharmacies have been identified.

Source BC Ministry of Health official news release, February 19, 2025. news.gov.bc.ca/releases/2025HLTH0008-000127
3
$1.698 billion in PharmaCare expenditure in 2023/24 — across 46.64 million paid claims
$1.698B

Total BC PharmaCare expenditure in 2023/24 was $1.698 billion across 46.64 million paid claims for 1.01 million beneficiaries. PharmaNet — BC's province-wide pharmacy network — records every dispense in BC community pharmacies. Despite this infrastructure, the Ministry's own investigative unit found systematic fraud across more than 60 pharmacies that PharmaNet data alone did not prevent.

Source BC Ministry of Health, PharmaCare Trends 2023/2024. Published by Pharmaceutical, Laboratory and Blood Services Division. gov.bc.ca
4
BC forced to overhaul safe supply program — moving to witnessed dosing by December 2025
Program overhaul

In December 2025, BC required all prescribed alternatives clients to transition to witnessed dosing — meaning patients must consume medications under direct supervision of a health professional. This policy reversal was a direct response to the confirmed diversion of prescribed opioids at scale. The BC government explicitly acknowledged that the fee structure for pharmacies had created financial incentives for bad actors to attract and retain patients fraudulently.

Source BC Ministry of Health, Access to Prescribed Alternatives in British Columbia: Policy Direction, December 2, 2025. gov.bc.ca
📋

Context note: The BC findings are primarily drawn from the 2025 Ministry of Health investigation into prescribed alternatives diversion — a specific program within PharmaCare. The broader prescription drug verification gap (duplicate prescriptions, cross-system fraud, eligibility verification) remains a separate and unresolved issue across all PharmaCare plans. BC's PharmaNet provides transaction data but not real-time cross-party verification of the kind Medisyns provides.

What Medisyns provides for British Columbia

The cross-party verification layer PharmaNet cannot provide alone

BC has PharmaNet — a province-wide dispensing record system. But PharmaNet records transactions; it does not verify eligibility across parties in real time before dispensing occurs. The Ministry's own investigation found that systematic fraud at 60+ pharmacies went undetected until a Special Investigative Unit intervened. Medisyns sits at the verification layer — every fill is cross-checked against the prescriber registry, insurer eligibility, and cross-pharmacy duplication before the drug is dispensed.

🛡

Pre-dispense verification

PharmaNet records what happened. Medisyns verifies before it happens — catching incentive schemes and duplicate fills at the point of authorization.

🔗

Cross-party eligibility

BC has multiple PharmaCare plans plus private insurers. Medisyns connects all parties to a single real-time verification event per fill.

Audit trail beyond PharmaNet

Every transaction logged on a permissioned ledger — immutable, timestamped, and auditable by the Ministry, College of Pharmacists, and law enforcement.
